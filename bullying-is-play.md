## 9. The Three Thread Collapses (Deepened)

Bullying is **play that collapses three critical threads**. Understanding this changes everything.

### 1. **CONSENT collapses to "Yes" without asking**

**Healthy play:**
- "Do you want to play?"
- "Can I join?"
- "Is this okay with you?"
- Both people's consent actively negotiated
- "No" is honored
- Reciprocal checking-in

**Collapsed play (bullying):**
- "You're playing whether you want to or not"
- The Other's "no" is invisible, unsafe, or socially inconvenient
- One-sided consent assumption
- Peer laughter overrides target's distress signals

**What collapsed:** The thread of **CONSENT (Within/Between)** — mutual recognition of both people's boundaries

**How S.A.F.E. restores it:**
- **ASK:** "What do you need?" / "What do they need?" = Consent mechanism restored
- Thread restored: **CONSENT** + **UNCERTAINTY** (not knowing = space for Other)

---

### 2. **PAUSE collapses to "More" without checking in**

**Healthy play:**
- "Is this too much?"
- "Should we slow down?"
- "Do we need to check in?"
- Built-in escalation brakes
- Regular assessment: *Is this still okay?*
- Space to adjust intensity

**Collapsed play (bullying):**
- "More. Harder. Keep going until *I* decide to stop"
- No pause mechanism
- Escalation loop without reflection
- The "trance" of automatic play

**What collapsed:** The thread of **PAUSE (Now/Not Yet)** — the ability to slow down and assess

**How S.A.F.E. restores it:**
- **SEE:** "I SEE something happening" = Interrupt the automatic play loop
- **EMERGE:** "Who do you want to be right now?" = Space to choose
- Thread restored: **PAUSE (space to choose)** + **PRESENCE (Within/Between)**

---

### 3. **EMBODIMENT collapses to "Stay" without inclusive feedback**

**Healthy play:**
- "How does your body feel?"
- "What are you noticing?"
- "Are we both still having fun?"
- Both people's body signals matter
- Somatic feedback is honored
- Mutual regulation

**Collapsed play (bullying):**
- "Your body's signals don't count. Only mine do"
- Excitement/adrenaline in the bully overrides distress in the target
- "Everyone was laughing" (peer embodiment) drowns out target's freeze/fawn

**What collapsed:** The thread of **EMBODIMENT (Body Knows)** — inclusive somatic awareness

**How S.A.F.E. restores it:**

- Thread restored: **EMBODIMENT (Body Knows)**

## 1) Bullying is a peer-group “game,” not a 1:1 moral failure

Christina Salmivalli’s work is foundational here: bullying persists because it’s **socially organized**—with roles like assistants, reinforcers (laughing/cheering), outsiders, and defenders. Bullying is a group process and peer responses help maintain it. ([Wiley Online Library][1])

## 2) Bullying can be a *strategic* way to gain dominance, status, and resources

Research in the “strategic” / dominance / resource-control tradition shows that some bullying is **instrumental**—a means to social dominance. For example, work on bullying as strategic behavior finds bullies can be socially dominant and may use both **coercive and prosocial** strategies (a “bistrategic” profile). ([PubMed][2])

**Why this supports your frame:**
Kids keep doing what *works*. “Play” here includes status games; the “collapsed play” lens gives you a way to intervene without pretending the behavior is irrational.

## 3) “Just joking” is a known cognitive/moral mechanism, amplified by peer laughter

There’s published work explicitly discussing how peer reactions make it easier for a bully to frame harm as “just for fun” or “just joking.” One example: research on moral disengagement notes how when peers are smiling/laughing, it becomes easier to interpret the act as joking. ([PubMed Central][3])

And there’s also research focused on the difficulty adults have distinguishing joking/teasing from harassment/bullying because “just joking” can be socially complex. ([SAGE Journals][4])

**Why this supports your frame:**
Your claim that bullying is “play under distorted conditions” aligns with known mechanisms: laughter + status + moral disengagement = “it’s not serious.”

## 4) Teasing really *does* sit on a continuum from playful to harmful

Recent work (2025) explicitly frames teasing along a **continuum** where “playful” and “harmful” share features, and playful intent can still cause harm—especially depending on relationship context and the target’s experience. ([Springer][5])

Also, youth themselves often distinguish teasing vs bullying using cues like “I know they’re joking,” but those cues are unstable and socially loaded—captured in qualitative research. ([ERIC][6])

**Why this supports your frame:**
This is your “feedback/embodiment” collapse in academic clothing: the *actor’s* intent doesn’t determine the *receiver’s* harm, and “play” without robust check-in norms flips into injury fast.

There’s evidence (at least in animal models) that aggressive social interaction can engage reward pathways—i.e., it can be experienced as pleasurable/reinforcing. ([Mount Sinai Today][7])

**Important nuance:** translating directly from animal models to human school bullying should be done carefully. But it’s still useful as “there are biological reinforcement mechanisms that can make dominance/aggression feel rewarding,” which helps explain why moral instruction alone fails.
