# S.A.F.E. For All

**An anti-bullying framework built on Threads of Becoming**

See. Ask. Feel. Emerge.

---

## The Core Insight

Most anti-bullying programs fail because they collapse into the very binary they're trying to solve: good kid/bad kid, victim/bully, safe/unsafe.

**S.A.F.E. works differently.** It teaches everyoneâ€”the kid being hurt, the kid doing harm, and the witnessesâ€”how to hold the tension of difficult moments without collapsing into shame, blame, or helplessness.

**When a child learns to be S.A.F.E., they're not learning rules. They're learning who they want to become when things get hard.**

## S.A.F.E.: See. Ask. Feel. Emerge.

### S - See What's Happening

**Notice what's happening in your body and around you**

**What this means:**
- Your heart is beating fast
- Your face feels hot
- Someone is saying words that hurt
- Someone is being left out
- Your hands want to push

**Thread Foundation:** PRESENCE (Within / Between) + EMBODIMENT (Body knows)

**For the kid being hurt:**
"I see that I'm being left out. I see that my stomach hurts."

**For the kid doing harm:**
"I see that I'm about to say something mean. I see that I want them to feel small."

**For the witness:**
"I see someone being hurt. I see that I'm scared to help."

**Why this is transformative:**
Most programs tell kids "don't bully" or "tell an adult." S.A.F.E. teaches presence firstâ€”noticing what's happening in your body and around you before reacting.

When kids learn to SEE (both internally and externally), they develop **awareness before action**â€”the foundation of all self-regulation and empathy.

---

### A - Ask About the Tension

**What's the push-pull? What are both sides pulling toward?**

Instead of "Who's the bad guy?" we ask "What tension is alive here?"

**Tension Examples:**

**Exclusion:**
- "I want to belong / I want to be myself"
- "I want my special friends / I know how it feels to be left out"

**Physical aggression:**
- "I'm angry / I don't want to hurt people"
- "I want respect / I don't want to solve this with fists"

**Relational aggression:**
- "I want power / I don't want to be mean"
- "I want them to like me / I can't control what others think"

**For witnesses:**
- "I want to help / I'm scared"
- "I want to fit in / I know this is wrong"

**Thread Foundation:** All seven threads show up in bullying dynamicsâ€”PRESENCE, CONSENT, MEMORY, PAUSE, EMBODIMENT, UNCERTAINTY, BECOMING

**Why this is transformative:**
Traditional programs frame conflict as "victim vs. bully." ASK reframes it as tensionâ€”two competing needs or values pulling against each other.

When kids learn to ASK about the tension (not assign blame), they develop **complexity thinking**: "Both sides have needs. How do we hold that?"

---

### F - Feel Both Sides

**Can you feel both pulls at the same time? Can both be true?**

This is the hardest step and the most powerful.

**Can you hold opposing truths without one winning?**

**For the kid being hurt:**
- "You deserve to be included AND other kids get to choose their friends. Both true?"
- "You want them to stop AND you can't control what they do. Can you hold both?"
- "You're brave AND you're scared. Both at the same time?"

**For the kid doing harm:**
- "You feel powerful when you're mean AND you don't like who you're being. Both true?"
- "You're angry at them AND anger doesn't mean you get to hurt them. Can you feel both?"
- "You want your friends to think you're tough AND being tough doesn't mean making others small. Both?"

**For the witness:**
- "You want to help AND you're scared of becoming a target. Both real?"
- "You care about your friend being hurt AND you don't want to lose your other friends. Can both matter?"

**Thread Foundation:** HOLD (the core practice of holding tension without collapse)

**Why this is transformative:**
When kids learn to FEEL both sides, they stop collapsing into victim mentality ("I'm helpless"), aggressor mentality ("They deserve it"), or bystander paralysis ("There's nothing I can do").

Instead they develop **agency within complexity**: "This is hard AND I can choose who I want to be."

---

### E - Emerge as Who You Want to Be

**Who do you want to be in this moment? What kind of person are you becoming?**

This isn't "what should you do" (rules). It's "who are you becoming" (identity formation).

**For the kid being hurt:**
- "Do you want to be someone who falls apart when one person is mean? Or someone who can feel hurt AND still have a good day?"
- "Do you want to be someone who needs everyone to like them? Or someone who knows they're valuable even when excluded?"

**For the kid doing harm:**
- "Do you want to be someone who gets power by making others small? Or someone who can feel big without making others feel little?"
- "When you're angry, who do you become? Is that who you want to be?"

**For the witness:**
- "Do you want to be someone who watches others get hurt because you're scared? Or someone who can be scared AND still do the right thing?"

**Thread Foundation:** BECOMING (Who I've been / Who I'm becoming) + UNCERTAINTY (Mystery unveiling)

**Why this is transformative:**
It shifts from external control ("You're in trouble") to internal formation ("You're becoming someone").

The kid learns: **I'm not a victim or a bully. I'm a person learning who I want to become.**

## How S.A.F.E. Creates Safety for Everyone

### For the Kid Being Hurt

**Traditional approach:**
"Tell an adult." "Ignore them." "Stand up for yourself."

**Why it fails:**
Kid learns: I'm helpless and need adults to fix my problems.

**S.A.F.E. approach:**
- **SEE:** "I see I'm being left out and my stomach hurts."
- **ASK:** "What's the tension? I want to belong AND they want their special friends."
- **FEEL:** "Both can be true. I can want to belong AND they can choose."
- **EMERGE:** "I can be someone who feels hurt AND makes a different choice (find new friends, do something I love, ask to join without needing them to say yes)."

**Result:**
Kid learns: I have feelings AND I have choices. I'm not helpless.

---

### For the Kid Doing Harm

**Traditional approach:**
"Stop bullying." Consequences. Punishment. Shame.

**Why it fails:**
Kid learns: I'm a bad person. Authority figures control me through fear.

**S.A.F.E. approach:**
- **SEE:** "I see I want to say something mean to make them feel small."
- **ASK:** "What's the tension? I want to feel powerful AND I don't like who I'm becoming when I'm mean."
- **FEEL:** "Both are true. I can want power AND not hurt others to get it."
- **EMERGE:** "Who do I want to be? Someone who gets power by making others small, or someone who feels strong without that?"

**Result:**
Kid learns: I can choose who I'm becoming. I'm not defined by my worst moment.

---

### For the Witness

**Traditional approach:**
"Be an upstander." "Tell an adult." "Don't let it happen."

**Why it fails:**
Kid learns: If I don't act (and I'm scared to act), I'm a bad person too.

**S.A.F.E. approach:**
- **SEE:** "I see someone being hurt. I see I'm scared to help."
- **ASK:** "What's the tension? I want to help AND I'm scared I'll be next."
- **FEEL:** "Both are real. I can care about them AND be scared."
- **EMERGE:** "Who do I want to be? Someone who stays quiet because of fear, or someone who can be scared AND still try (ask if they're okay, sit with them at lunch, tell a teacher what I saw)?"

**Result:**
Kid learns: Courage isn't the absence of fear. It's choosing who I want to be even when I'm scared.

---

### For the Teacher

**Traditional approach:**
Solve the conflict. Assign consequences. Separate kids. "Be nice to each other."

**Why it fails:**
Kids don't learn to navigate tensionâ€”they learn to avoid getting caught.

**S.A.F.E. approach:**
Teacher facilitates, doesn't solve:
- **SEE:** "What do you notice happening? What do you feel in your body?"
- **ASK:** "What's the tension here? What are both of you needing?"
- **FEEL:** "Can both of those things be true at the same time?"
- **EMERGE:** "Who do each of you want to be in this moment?"

**Result:**
Kids develop internal capacity for conflict navigation. Teacher becomes facilitator of growth, not enforcer of rules.

## How S.A.F.E. Is Different from Traditional Programs

| **Traditional Programs** | **S.A.F.E.** |
|--------------------------|--------------|
| **Zero-tolerance policies** (punishment-based) | **Tension-holding** (learning-based) |
| **Victim/bully binary** (good kid/bad kid) | **Everyone's becoming** (we're all learning who we want to be) |
| **Adult intervention** ("Tell a teacher") | **Student agency** ("I can choose who I'm becoming") |
| **Stop the behavior** (external control) | **Build capacity** (internal formation) |
| **Rules to follow** ("Don't bully") | **Identity to form** ("Who do I want to be?") |
| **Reactive** (respond after harm happens) | **Preventive** (practice daily in all conflicts) |
| **Program to implement** (added to school day) | **Language to speak** (integrated into culture) |

## Teacher Training: S.A.F.E. Facilitation

### Module 1: Understanding the Threads (3 hours)
- Overview of seven Threads of Becoming
- How threads show up in bullying dynamics
- Personal thread work: Where do I collapse?

### Module 2: The Four S.A.F.E. Steps (3 hours)
- Deep dive into See, Ask, Feel, Emerge
- Practicing with real scenarios
- When S.A.F.E. is appropriate vs. when crisis intervention is needed

### Module 3: Facilitation Practice (3 hours)
- Role-playing student conflicts
- How to hold space without solving
- Questions that invite reflection, not answers

### Module 4: Classroom Integration (3 hours)
- Daily S.A.F.E. practices (morning check-ins, literature connections, recess debriefs)
- Age-appropriate adaptations (K-2, 3-5, 6-8)
- Parent communication strategies

### Module 5: Ongoing Support (monthly, 1 hour)
- Case consultation with school counselor
- Thread study with colleagues
- Reflection on facilitation challenges

**Total: 12 hours initial training + 9 hours/year ongoing support**
